What are (Dynamic) Fualt Trees

Proper risk analysis is essential to keep our power plants, trains and self-driving cars safe and operational. The increased constraints imposed by international standards together with the ever-growing penetration of AI components in high-tech systems make rigorous and powerful risk assessment more important than ever.
Various techniques have been developed throughout the years to analyse the safety and reliability of systems. One of the most relevant is Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) (Stamatelatos et al., 2002; Ruijters and Stoelinga, 2015). Every day, millions of engineers are using fault trees to model their systems and analyse the probability of failures. FTA is applied to many safety-critical systems and their use is required for instance by the Federal Aviation Authority (FAA), the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC), in ISO 26262 for autonomous driving or for software development in aerospace systems (by NASA and ESA).
While fault trees are easy to understand and thus, widely used, their modelling capabilities are severely limited. This lack of flexibility hinders accurate and precise modelling of real-life systems. To overcome these drawbacks, several extensions of fault trees have been proposed in the last decades. Dynamic fault trees (DFT) (Dugan et al., 1992) are a prominent extension of fault trees overcoming these limitations. DFTs have been co-developed by NASA to model more complex behaviours and dependability patterns. As such, DFTs can capture more realistic scenarios needed to handle the growing complexity in, e.g., self-driving cars, hyperloops and drones.
While fault tree models represent how failures occur at system component level and how they propagate through sub-systems, eventually leading to system level failures, their analysis focuses on computing various dependability metrics, i.e. key performance indicators that measure how well a system performs. Standard metrics are the system reliability (i.e. the probability that no failure occurred until time T), the availability (i.e. the average percentage of time that a system is operational), the mean time to failure, the mean time between failures, the criticality if components (i.e. to what extent does a component failure contribute to a system failure) etc. Various extensions include the cost and impact of failures

Why do we need Dynamic Fault Trees

The need for rigorous risk assessments is rapidly increasing. On the one hand, international standards -- require rigorous and powerful fault tree analysis techniques. For instance, the recent ISO 26262 standard in autonomous driving asserts that “metrics are verifiable and precise enough to differentiate between different system architectures” and that “[for systems where the] concept is based on redundant safety mechanisms, multiple-point failures of a higher order than two are considered in the analysis” (ISO, 2011). Classical fault-tree analysis techniques cannot support the compliance of products to these requirements. Alternative, and more powerful means are needed that allow flexible modelling, a rich set of metrics, and powerful techniques for advanced analyses such as multiple-point failures of higher degrees.
On the other hand, the rapidly increased usage of AI components in modern systems necessitates a rigorous risk assessment. Neural networks typically come with weak statistical guarantees only (if at all). Small perturbations of their inputs may lead to misclassifications that can be catastrophic in safety-critical applications: consider an AI component in an autonomous car that misclassifies a pedestrian who is crossing the street at an arbitrary place (and not at a cross-over). The use of AI components also implies the need for reliability metrics that go beyond the standard metrics of reliability and availability. In particular, there is a need to specify various levels of degradation (given the uncertainty of the AI components) and be able to analyse e.g., “what is the probability that the system will come to a halt without going through degradation level A first”.
Thus, the increased constraints imposed by international standards together with the ever-growing penetration of AI components make rigorous and powerful risk assessment more important than ever.

New Approach to DFT Analysis

The increased modeling power of DFTs comes at a price DFTs are more complex to analyse. This is mainly due to the fact that DFTs incorporate dynamic behaviour, where also the order in which failures occur is important. This significantly increases the number of distinct behaviours which need to be analysed. Various techniques to analyse DFTs have been proposed before (Tang and Dugan, 2004; Montani et al., 2008; Rauzy, 2011), but the majority does not scale to industry-size fault trees, which may comprise hundreds or thousands of fault tree gates.
RWTH Aachen University Germany and Twente University the Netherlands have worked on a new fully automated approach to DFT analysis, namely via stochastic model checking. Stochastic model checking (Baier and Katoen, 2008) is a state-of-the-art analysis technique offering a wide plethora of powerful analysis techniques for stochastic systems. State space reduction techniques, such as symmetry reduction, bisimulation minimization (Baier and Katoen, 2008, Chap. 7), reduce one model to a smaller one that is computationally equivalent. Approximative techniques allow one to approximate analysis results upto arbitrary precision.
Several advanced methods (Ruijters et al., 2016; Ruijters et al., 2017, Volk et al., 2018) have been developed to analyze DFTs that apart from increasing the computational efficiency also provide more modeling power. Further, a wide variety of different metrics are offered, analyzing the system reliability under various scenarios. The combined power of these methods allows us to analyse DFTs that were out of scope before (Katoen and Stoelinga, 2017).
The research results were published in highly ranked journals from both computer science and engineering. For instance, our survey paper on fault trees (Ruijters and Stoelinga, 2015) was cited over 450 times. The publication presenting the underlying model checker (Dehnert et al., 2017) was published in the highest ranked conference in the field of formal verification and cited over 300 times

Industrial applications of DFT

Several industrial organizations ProRail, NS, Movares, Delta Pi, ESA, the Nuclear Research Group, Arcadis, Thales Alennia Space, Space Systems Finland, Ford, BMW and EDF have participated in academic projects to figure out the real potential of DFT. Together with these companies, the powerful modelling capabilities of DFT and the feasibility of our analysis approaches have been demonstrated in a range of industrial applications. Example applications include railway systems (Ruijters et al., 2016; Volk et al., 2019), smart buildings (Cauchi et al., 2017), autonomous driving (Ghadhab et al., 2019), aerospace systems (Esteve et al., 2012), and energy networks (Khan et al., 2019).
These applications supported the involved companies in their decision making. For example, the cost analysis in (Ruijters et al., 2016) was used by ProRail when pursuing a new railroad joint, comparing the costs and reliability of various joint types. The experiences with the analysis of the HVAC helped the Dutch Railways (NS) to introduce fault tree analysis for various assets. The case study (Ghadhab et al, 2019) supported BMW in performing an extensive design space exploration, comparing several mappings from software onto different hardware architectures.
